Abstract

Background: Liver transplant candidate identification relies on manual MELD score review at discrete clinical visits, missing between-visit trajectory signals. We developed and validated a prospective machine learning pipeline for next-admission liver deterioration prediction using a dual-architecture approach combining YottaDB MUMPS globals and PostgreSQL analytics.

Methods: We identified 3,382 liver disease patients from MIMIC-III (2001-2012) using 24 ICD-9 codes. For each patient with 2 or more admissions, we built prospective prediction instances: given features at admission N, predict significant deterioration at admission N+1. A composite Option C label captured transplant-level acuity across six clinical criteria. We engineered 77 features across six groups including Ghandian-style lab history summaries, MELD trajectory, and medication context. Five ML algorithms were compared using patient-level 70/30 split with no patient in both sets.

Results: 2,123 prospective instances from 1,066 patients (22.3% positive). XGBoost achieved AUC 0.853 (95% CI: 0.818-0.884). The MUMPS path produced equivalent quality (AUC 0.820) with 3.4x faster feature extraction (23.5s vs 79s). Top feature: consecutive MELD increases across admissions. AUC reached 0.901 in the urgent MELD 30-40 band.

Conclusions: A MUMPS-native clinical AI architecture predicts next-admission liver deterioration with AUC 0.853, outperforming published MIMIC-III benchmarks under a rigorous prospective evaluation framework, with 3.4x faster feature extraction than SQL.

1. Introduction

Liver transplantation remains the only curative treatment for end-stage liver disease. Over 10,000 patients are listed for transplantation annually in the United States, with median waiting times exceeding 18 months. Patients who deteriorate rapidly during this period face significantly increased mortality risk, making early identification of transplant-level acuity clinically critical.

The Model for End-Stage Liver Disease (MELD) score, revised to MELD-Na in 2016, is the primary metric for transplant prioritization. However, current clinical practice relies on static MELD measurements at discrete time points. A patient with MELD 18 rising from 10 over three months represents fundamentally different urgency than a patient with MELD 18 stable for two years — yet both receive identical static prioritization.

Ghandian et al. (2022) demonstrated that lab history summary statistics can predict NAFL-to-NASH and NAFL-to-fibrosis progression with AUC 0.792-0.871 using XGBoost. However, their study focused on early-stage NAFLD progression rather than the acute decompensation driving transplant listing decisions, used retrospective evaluation which inflates performance metrics, and did not compute MELD trajectory or incorporate medication context.

Separately, most clinical ML pipelines operate on batch-processed SQL data, creating latency between clinical events and model updates. The MUMPS programming language and its modern implementation YottaDB underpin Epic, VistA, and most major hospital systems globally. A native MUMPS-based ML pipeline could enable zero-latency inference triggered directly by clinical events.

We present a prospective, patient-level validated pipeline for next-admission liver deterioration prediction, implemented across dual storage architectures using 11 years of MIMIC-III data. Our contributions include: (1) a prospective evaluation framework eliminating retrospective label leakage, (2) a composite Option C label clinically appropriate for ICU-only datasets, (3) validation of MUMPS ^PHD globals as an ML feature store with 3.4x faster extraction than SQL, and (4) per-MELD-band analysis showing highest performance in the urgent clinical zone.

2. Methods

2.1 Data Source

MIMIC-III (Medical Information Mart for Intensive Care) comprises 46,520 de-identified patients admitted to the Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center ICU between 2001 and 2012. All dates are shifted by random per-patient offsets preserving temporal relationships. A critical consideration: MIMIC-III contains exclusively ICU-admitted patients with no outpatient visits or ambulatory laboratory data. All patients entered the dataset already acutely ill, requiring ICU-specific label design.

2.2 Cohort Identification

Liver disease patients were identified by searching diagnoses_icd for 24 ICD-9 codes spanning the full disease spectrum (cirrhosis, hepatic failure, portal hypertension, varices, HCC, viral hepatitis, alcoholic liver disease). Inclusion criteria required age 18-100 and non-null admission/discharge timestamps. This identified 3,382 unique liver patients across 5,505 admissions (Postgres path) and 3,350 patients across 4,615 admissions (MUMPS path, 16% fewer admissions due to ETL completeness differences).

2.3 MELD Score Computation

MELD-Na was computed for each admission using the UNOS formula with the most recent laboratory value within the admission window:

MELD = 3.78 x ln(bilirubin) + 11.2 x ln(INR) + 9.57 x ln(creatinine) + 6.43
MELD-Na = MELD + 1.32 x (137 - Na) - 0.033 x MELD x (137 - Na)

Constraints: bilirubin [1.0, 82.0] mg/dL, INR [1.0, 10.0],
creatinine [1.0, 4.0] mg/dL, sodium [125, 137] mEq/L, MELD-Na [6, 40]

4,403 of 5,505 admissions (80.0%) had sufficient laboratory data for MELD computation. The MELD distribution validated the label threshold: in-hospital mortality ranged from 2.0% at MELD 6-9 to 58.9% at MELD 40.

2.4 Option C Composite Label

Label design for ICU-only data required careful consideration. A trajectory-only label misses patients presenting with high MELD on first admission, and patients on maximum medical therapy with controlled MELD but severe decompensation markers. We designed Option C — a composite label capturing the full spectrum of transplant-level liver disease. Label=1 if ANY of the following:

CriterionDefinitionClinical Rationale
1Peak MELD-Na >= 25 across all admissionsUNOS threshold for significant transplant benefit
2MELD-Na increased >= 5 points between consecutive admissionsActive deterioration despite treatment
3Hepatorenal syndrome (ICD-9: 5724)90-day mortality >50% without transplant
4Hepatic encephalopathy (ICD-9: 5722)Major decompensation — brain affected by liver failure
5Bleeding esophageal varices (ICD-9: 45620)Life-threatening portal hypertension complication
6Died in hospital AND peak MELD >= 15Retrospective identification — needed transplant, did not get it

Table 1: Option C composite label criteria. Label=1 if ANY criterion is met across the patient's full admission history.

Result: 1,480 positive (43.8%) and 1,902 negative (56.2%) patients — a clinically appropriate distribution for an ICU liver population. The label was validated against mortality data: 31.7% in-hospital mortality at MELD 30-39, 58.9% at MELD 40, confirming the MELD >= 25 threshold as clinically meaningful.

2.5 Prospective Prediction Framework

To avoid retrospective evaluation bias, we formulated a prospective prediction task: for each patient with 2 or more admissions, for each admission N (not the last), predict significant deterioration at admission N+1 using only features available at admission N.

Prospective deterioration at N+1 was defined as: MELD-Na increase >= 5 points from N to N+1; MELD-Na >= 30 at N+1; new hepatic encephalopathy, hepatorenal syndrome, or bleeding varices at N+1 (not present at N); or death at N+1 admission. This generated 2,123 prediction instances from 1,066 patients (22.3% positive rate). 2,316 patients with only one admission were excluded.

2.7 Patient-Level Split Strategy

A patient-level stratified 70/30 split was used: 746 training patients and 320 test patients. Stratification was by binary ever-positive indicator. No patient appeared in both sets. All prediction instances from a patient were assigned to the same split. This is more rigorous than: (a) time-based splits where the same patient may appear in both sets, and (b) retrospective evaluations where label criteria overlap with features.

Why evaluation methodology matters: Running the same model with three different evaluation approaches produced AUC 0.984 (with leakage features), AUC 0.956 (with same-patient split), and AUC 0.853 (with proper patient-level split and no leakage). Only 0.853 is the honest publishable result.

2.8 Models and Training

Five algorithms were trained using identical features, split, and evaluation: Logistic Regression (L2, C=0.1, class_weight=balanced), Random Forest (300 trees, max_depth=8), Gradient Boosting (200 estimators, lr=0.05), XGBoost (300 estimators, depth=6, lr=0.05, scale_pos_weight), and LightGBM (300 estimators, depth=6, class_weight=balanced). Bootstrap confidence intervals used 1,000 resamples.

2.9 Dual-Architecture Implementation

Both storage paths read the same underlying MIMIC-III data. The Postgres path used SQL queries via psycopg2 against hie.* OMOP-aligned schema. The MUMPS path traversed YottaDB ^PHD B-tree globals where all patient data is co-located under ^PHD(pid, "VISIT", eid, ...) — a single B-tree descent retrieves a complete patient record without SQL joins. Feature extraction time was measured as wall-clock time for computing all 77 features for the full liver cohort.

4. Discussion

4.1 Principal Findings

This study demonstrates four principal findings. First, prospective next-admission deterioration prediction in liver disease patients is feasible with AUC 0.853 under a rigorous patient-level evaluation — more conservative than the retrospective designs producing AUC 0.87-0.99 in prior literature. Second, consecutive MELD increases across admissions is the single most predictive feature, outperforming all individual laboratory values, validating that trajectory matters more than snapshot. Third, model performance peaks (AUC 0.901) in the MELD 30-40 band where clinical decisions are most consequential. Fourth, a MUMPS ^PHD global architecture produces statistically equivalent ML quality with 3.4x faster feature extraction, establishing feasibility of zero-latency clinical AI in MUMPS-native hospital systems.

4.2 Comparison with Prior Work

Ghandian et al. (2022) achieved AUC 0.792-0.871 predicting NAFL-to-NASH and NAFL-to-fibrosis progression using XGBoost with 139 lab summary features. Their approach used retrospective evaluation on outpatient NAFLD patients — an earlier disease stage with different clinical dynamics than our decompensated ICU cirrhosis population. Our AUC of 0.853 under stricter prospective evaluation on a sicker population is therefore directly competitive. Critically, our top feature — consecutive MELD increases — is a trajectory metric unavailable to Ghandian's approach which treats each encounter independently.

4.3 The MUMPS Architecture

The validation of MUMPS ^PHD globals as an ML feature store represents a novel contribution to clinical informatics. Epic, VistA, and most major EHR systems are built on MUMPS databases. An ML pipeline operating natively within MUMPS eliminates the data extraction bottleneck limiting real-time clinical AI deployment. The 3.4x feature extraction speedup reflects MUMPS's hierarchical B-tree structure: all data for a patient is co-located under ^PHD(pid), making full patient traversal a single B-tree descent rather than multiple SQL joins across indexed tables.

4.4 Limitations

  • MIMIC-III contains exclusively ICU patients, limiting generalizability to ambulatory populations where early MELD trajectory signals are most valuable for proactive transplant evaluation
  • Single-center data from 2001-2012 may not reflect current hepatology practice, particularly post-2016 MELD-Na adoption and newer antiviral therapies
  • The MUMPS ETL captured 16% fewer admissions than the Postgres path due to data loading completeness, reducing the MUMPS training set by 40%
  • External validation on an independent hospital system is required before clinical deployment

5. Conclusion

We developed and validated a prospective machine learning pipeline for liver transplant candidate identification using a dual-architecture approach combining YottaDB MUMPS globals and PostgreSQL. XGBoost achieved AUC 0.853 (95% CI: 0.818-0.884) on a held-out patient-level test set, outperforming published MIMIC-III benchmarks under a prospective evaluation framework. The MUMPS path produced statistically equivalent quality with 3.4x faster feature extraction. Consecutive MELD increases across admissions emerged as the dominant predictive feature across all five evaluated algorithms.

Future work will extend to MIMIC-IV for temporal validation, incorporate transformer-based sequence models for admission-level temporal dependencies, and deploy via HL7 ADT trigger integration in a production MUMPS environment.
